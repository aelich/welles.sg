WHY DOES FACEBOOK WANT LIBRA

08 Nov 2019

  • Introduction
    Welles Partners thinks Facebook’s Libra is a game changer in the cryptocurrency world. The following article is the first of a series which explains why.

    Why would Facebook design a cryptocurrency it will not fully control?
    According to David Marcus who runs the whole Libra show for Facebook, it started during a holiday when he realized that building a protocol for money was the way to go, if he wanted to build a frictionless global digital currency for the many people with mobile phones but no access to banking services nor can afford the high costs of financial services, the so called Bottom of the pyramid.

    David Marcus’s hypothesis was that the current system had reached its limitations. Money networks are proprietary and not well interconnected. Of course there are interbank networks like SWIFT, central banks have their own networks, but all these systems were built in the 60s and 70s and were cracking at the seams.

    Current payment systems suffer from the limitations of the underlying networks, and no open standard for interoperability exists. Complexity piles up when money needs to be transferred from one country to another

    Time had come for a change, and not an incremental one.

    How would Facebook build the internet of money
    Facebook has long tried to build an in-house currency, the late Facebook Credit, but this time, with the lofty aspiration to empower consumers in the developing world, it was a different ball game, an ambitious plan that is meant to take over Bitcoin, and beat Apple, Google and WeChat companies that are making inroads into global financial markets.

    So Facebook summoned a group of more than 100 people, top engineers, top economists and policymakers to tackle a double challenge: on one side, build a global currency on top of the blockchain technology, a simple global currency and infrastructure that would empower billions of people, that would deliver services like micropayments and remittances without excessive fees, that would be easy to use as email, that would allow dynamic contracts to enable blockchain-based loans or insurances, and on the other side convince people to adopt it despite the fact that Facebook built it.

    Measure, justice and freedom
    The currency got its name Libra from an ancient Roman unit of measure, a weight measure but understood also as money, the group of stars in the Milky Way that is also the symbol of Justice, scales, and its resemblance with libre, the French word for freedom.

    To deflect the well-justified wariness of Facebook’s every move, the technology is open source, and the control of its evolution is ceded to a neutral “Libra Association”, much like well-known cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin or Ethereum that have created Foundations to steer the evolution of their currencies.

    An association of peers
    Facebook scrambled to recruit up to 100 founding members of the Libra Association, each of which will invest at least USD 10 million, to fund the Association’s operations and build a reserve basket of fiat currencies comprising up to 200 billion worth of dollars, yen, euros and pounds sterling. Libra is thus labeled a “stablecoin” as its value does not fluctuate with speculation or manipulation.

    So far less than 30 members have been recruited, some of which are Non Governmental Organizations like Kiva and Women’s World Banking, which are not contributing money but will offer financial services to the unbanked.

    So far the members of the Association who have pledged their participation, have not yet contributed funds. One prominent would be member, Paypal, has recently announced it would pull out of the Association altogether, citing heavy scrutiny and resistance from regulators, that distract them from their core business.

    Fiat currencies reserve basket
    At any given moment, a user will be able to convert her Libras in one of the existing currencies. To avoid speculation or currency manipulation, the reserve basket is built such that it covers every single Libra in circulation.

    This mechanism however can be a blessing as well as a curse, as this pegging will take away part of the ability for some governments to devalue their currencies.

    This may explain why a large number of regulators and central banks have expressed their concerns, which we will address in an article to come.
